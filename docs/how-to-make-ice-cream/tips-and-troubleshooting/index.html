Tips & Troubleshooting

Tips & Techniques ⏱ 2 min read

Even experienced ice cream makers run into problems. This page covers the most common issues and the simple fixes for them, plus a few techniques that will consistently improve your results.

1. Common Problems#

This is almost always caused by large ice crystals forming. The most common reasons:

  • Not enough fat — use double cream (36%+ fat), not single or whipping cream.
  • Too much water — make sure to use whole milk, not skimmed.
  • Insufficient churning — the ice cream machine needs enough time to incorporate air and freeze the mixture quickly.
  • Refreezing after melting — once ice cream melts, the crystal structure changes. Do not refreeze melted ice cream.

Ice cream that sets rock solid is usually caused by:

  • Too little sugar — sugar lowers the freezing point. Don't reduce it significantly below the recipe.
  • Too little fat — again, fat interferes with ice crystal growth and keeps things softer.
  • Freezer is too cold — most home ice cream freezes best at around -18°C (0°F). Remove from the freezer 5–10 minutes before serving.

Scrambled eggs in the custard happen when the temperature rises too fast or too high.

  • Always temper the eggs by adding a small amount of hot liquid slowly before combining everything.
  • Cook the custard over low heat, never boiling.
  • Use a thermometer — pull the custard off the heat at 75–80°C (167–176°F).

If it does scramble, you can sometimes rescue it by blending and straining thoroughly, but the texture will suffer.

Over-whipping the cream before folding in the other ingredients will turn it to butter. Whip only to soft peaks — the cream should hold its shape loosely but still look creamy, not stiff or grainy.

Cold temperatures dull our perception of sweetness and flavour. When testing a warm mixture, it will taste too sweet and intense — that is correct. You can compensate by slightly over-seasoning with vanilla or other flavourings before freezing.

2. Pro Tips#

  • Chill your custard overnight — the longer the base chills, the more the fat crystallises and the better the final texture after churning.
  • Pre-freeze your machine bowl — most home ice cream machines require the bowl to be frozen solid (24 hours) before use. Keep it in the freezer so it's always ready.
  • Add mix-ins at the last minute — stir in chocolate chips, fruit, or nuts in the final 2–3 minutes of churning, not at the start.
  • Cover the surface — always press cling film directly onto the surface of the ice cream before putting the lid on. This prevents ice crystals and freezer burn.
  • Use a warmed scoop — run your ice cream scoop under hot water before scooping for clean, smooth balls.

The amount of air churned into ice cream during freezing. More overrun gives a lighter, softer texture. Home machines typically produce 20–50% overrun.

Ice crystal formation
Back
When water in the ice cream mixture freezes into large crystals, producing a gritty texture. Prevented by high fat content, sufficient sugar, and rapid freezing.
Front
Creme anglaise
Back
The cooked custard base used in French-style ice cream — a mixture of egg yolks, sugar, milk, and cream cooked until thickened.
Front
No-churn method
Back
A technique using whipped cream and condensed milk instead of a custard base and ice cream machine. The whipped cream provides air, and the condensed milk keeps the texture soft.
